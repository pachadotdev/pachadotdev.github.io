Research

My main research interests and contributions are in three interrelated areas: (i) Political economy of trade policies; (ii) Gravity estimation and efficient software implementations; and (iii) Efficient data access, interrelated areas directly linked to the Sustainable Development goals of Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure and Peace, Justice and Strong Institutions.

For my thesis for the Master in Political Science, I am working on each of these topics, with a focus in reproducibility. I prepared a preprint on arXiv, Economic effects of Chile FTAs and an eventual CTPP accession, using aggregated data, to illustrate the general idea.

Political economy of trade policies

My main interest is to explore the distributional consequences of trade policy changes at industry and sector levels. The global economic interdependence justifies measuring the effect of trade policy changes in one market and how this will trigger ripple effects in the rest of the world. Economic gains or losses are not homogeneous at sub-national level, and are often highly correlated with industries or sectors geographically dispersed because of especialization.

I am interested in studying existing agreements, observed tariffs, non-tariff measures (NTMs), and bilateral trade flow to produce knowledge that countries can use to negociate or expand their agreements intelligently. My central questions are:

  • Which are the decomposed effects (i.e. GDP changes from one province) of the trade policy?
  • Which commodities should be present in tarrifs negotiation?
  • Which is the ideal timing to face trade-induced unemployment with industry protection and reconverting the workforce?
  • Can we use NTMs to achieving Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)?

Trade policy also covers sanctions, whose application aims at destabiling a dictatorship, preventing war or protecting human rights in a country. I ask myself about the efficacy of those measures and who finally pays the costs of those sanctions, but I also ask myself how to tailor sanctions to maximize their efectiveness.

Gravity estimation and efficient software implementations

One of the challenges in gravity modeling is to produce theoretically consistent models that are also computationally efficient. For masters in Statistics' thesis I replicated all the models in the book An Advanced Guide to Trade Policy Analysis and writing open source software for specific gravity modeling including clustered standard errors and misspecification tests.

In this line, I have written the R package gravity, with its corresponding article, which implements different methods available in the literature. It covers Poisson Pseudo Maximum Likelihood (PPML), OLS, Logit and other classic methods, with the idea of providing policy makers with easy to use software that allows them to focus on the evaluation of initiatives such as subscribing or expanding a Free Trade Agreement.

I write open source software because Least Developed Countries (LDCs) face limited resources for commercial software and computational power, and they lack the required analytical capacity to formulate evidence-based policies and assess their impacts. I believe I can make a contribution to strengthening analytical capacity of LDCs to conduct analysis of impact of trade policy towards sustainable development.

Efficient data access

I intend to summarise gigabytes of data into country and product profiles for academics and policymakers interested in trade. Since 2017, I’ve maintained the project Open Trade Statistics, where I provide free access to curated trade data based on the United Nations trade statistics.

I’ve created R packages to easily access The Design of Trade Agreements Database (DESTA), the International Trade and Production Database for Estimation (ITPD-E), and other affine datasets.

This line of work aims at easing data access to help to facilitate research, and has required me to master R, C++ and SQL, therefore I have computational skills more common in software engineering rather than Political Science or Statistics

LaTeX

I created the R package varsityblues, which allow you to write assignments, presentations and thesis in RStudio by following UofT style rules. Its advantage is that avoids the need to copy and paste tables or plots from R outputs into Word/LaTeX documents, instead it allows to work with notebooks that you can export to PDF, and it installs/configures LaTeX setup automatically.

This package is based on the LaTeX style files that that late professor Kim C. Border once sent to me.

Cloud computing

Datasets

Open Trade Statistics

Since 2017 I've maintained Open Trade Statistics, a project created with the intention to lower the barrier to working with international economic trade data. It includes a public API, a dashboard, and an R package for data retrieval. The project started when I was affected by the fact that many Latin American Universities have limited or no access to the United Nations Commodity Trade Statistics Database (UN COMTRADE).

OTS provides a curated database derived from UN COMTRADE. I can provide a full SQL dump of the database or share subsets of the database for the period 2002-2020 aggregated by countries or by products up to a detail of six digits in the Harmonised System revision 2012.

After contacting UN COMTRADE, and suggesting to them my idea of doing something similar to BACI but available for anyone but keeping commercial purposes out of the scope of the project, I got an authorization to share curated versions of their datasets.

SQL Databases for students and educators

Check SQL Databases for students and educators if you need access to PostgreSQL, MySQL and SQL Server databases (in strictly descending order of preference!).

Publicly accessible databases often impose query limits or require registration. Even when I maintain public and limit-free APIs, I never wanted to host a public database because I tend to think that the connection strings are a problem for the user.

I’ve decided to host different light/medium size by using PostgreSQL and MariaDB (a modern fork of MySQL) backends. I tend to prefer PostgreSQL because it easier to configure on client and server side, without strange tricks (see the Problems with MySQL/Maria and SQL Server section).

Why these database backends? I think there are a ton of small edge cases when moving between DB back ends and so testing lots with live databases is quite valuable. With this resource you can benchmark speed, compression, and DDL types.

Books

The Hitchhiker's Guide to Ggplot2 & The Hitchhiker's Guide to Plotnine

The Hitchhiker's Guide to Ggplot2 is a plotting book for R users. The Hitchhiker's Guide to Plotnine is a plotting book for Python users.

These are a technical books. The scope of the books is to go straight to the point and the writing style is similar to a recipe with detailed instructions. It is assumed that you know the basics of R and Python and that you want to learn how to create beautiful plots.

Each chapter will explain how to create a different type of plot, and will take you step-by-step from a basic plot to a highly customised graph. The chapters' order is by degree of difficulty.

Every chapter is independent from the others. You can read the whole book or go to a section of interest and we are sure that it will be easy to understand the instructions and reproduce our examples without reading the first chapters.
